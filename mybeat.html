Halsey

I really love how Halsey effortlessly blends different genres like pop, alternative, and electropop, creating a sound that's distinctly her own. Her music has this raw, unfiltered quality that makes it feel deeply personal yet widely relatable. Albums like Badlands and Manic feel like invitations into her mind—intimate glimpses of her struggles, triumphs, and vulnerabilities. There’s an honesty in her lyrics that makes it easy to connect with her, whether she’s exploring themes of identity, heartbreak, or mental health. Her advocacy for mental health awareness and LGBTQ+ rights also resonates with me; it’s admirable how she uses her platform for issues that genuinely matter. It’s not just about the music—Halsey creates a space for people to feel seen and heard. Every time I listen to her, I feel a genuine connection, like I’m not just hearing a song but a story that was meant to be shared.

Taylor Swift

I've always admired Taylor Swift's ability to reinvent herself while remaining true to her core as a storyteller. She’s journeyed through country, pop, indie-folk, and even alternative sounds, each era bringing a new layer of depth to her artistry. Her country roots in Fearless and Speak Now captured the innocence of young love, while the synth-pop vibes of 1989 showcased a vibrant, self-assured version of herself. Then there’s the introspective, indie-folk style of Folklore and Evermore, which felt like a comforting conversation during a time of uncertainty. Her lyrics have this poetic, almost diary-like quality that can transport me back to moments of heartbreak, growth, and self-reflection. Beyond her music, I respect how vocal she is about artists' rights and gender equality—using her platform to challenge industry norms. Taylor's fearlessness in reclaiming her work and advocating for creative ownership shows her strength not just as an artist but as a person. Her music doesn’t just tell stories; it captures entire eras of emotion.

Fiona Apple

Fiona Apple's music holds a depth and complexity that I rarely find elsewhere. Her blend of alternative, art pop, and baroque pop creates a soundscape that feels both intricate and unrestrained—like organized chaos. Fetch the Bolt Cutters was more than just an album for me; it felt like a reckoning, a declaration of freedom and defiance. There's an authenticity to her music that doesn't try to cater to the mainstream but instead challenges listeners to sit with discomfort and confront raw, unfiltered emotion. Her voice, unconventional and emotive, paired with her haunting piano work, always leaves me feeling both unsettled and understood. Fiona’s willingness to be unapologetically complex and to embrace imperfection is something I deeply admire. Her exploration of trauma, anger, and introspection is layered in a way that makes each listen a new experience. It’s not easy music, but it’s genuine, and that’s what makes it resonate so deeply with me.

Lana Del Rey

Lana Del Rey's music has this dreamy, cinematic quality that feels timeless yet contemporary—a blend of nostalgia and melancholy. Her ability to merge indie pop, baroque pop, and dream pop results in songs that are not just melodies but entire atmospheres. When I listen to Born to Die or Norman F**ing Rockwell!, it feels like being transported into a hazy, vintage film—full of romance, tragedy, and longing. Her lyrics are poetic yet accessible, often tinged with a bittersweetness that makes you feel like you’re part of the narrative. There’s a vulnerability in her work that balances the romanticization of love and loss with an awareness of their darker realities. I admire how she plays with contradictions—beauty and decay, love and loneliness—crafting an aesthetic that’s uniquely hers. Lana's style is so distinctive and evocative; it’s like she’s built a world that you can only fully understand when you let yourself sink into the sadness and sweetness of her music.
